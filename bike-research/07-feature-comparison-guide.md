# Feature Comparison Guide — What Matters for Your Ironman

Not a spec sheet. Real explanations of what each feature actually means for you on the 180km Alpe d'Huez bike leg.

---

## 1. Frame Material: Carbon vs Aluminium

![Bicycle frame diagram](images/bicycle-diagram.svg)

| Factor | Carbon | Aluminium |
|---|---|---|
| **Weight** | 8.0-9.2 kg | 9.0-10.5 kg |
| **Vibration damping** | Excellent — carbon soaks up road buzz | Poor — aluminium transmits vibration |
| **Fatigue over 5h** | Less fatiguing | More fatiguing |
| **Crash repair** | Can crack — expensive repair/replace | Can dent — but usually rideable |
| **Price** | €1,500+ (new) | €600+ (new) |
| **Warranty** | 6 years typical | Lifetime on most frames |

**Verdict for your Ironman:** Carbon is worth it. After 150km on rough alpine roads, the vibration damping of carbon will leave you fresher for the marathon. The weight difference (1-2 kg) helps on the Alpe d'Huez climb (13.8km at 8% average).

**Drawback of carbon:** If you crash and crack the frame, replacement is expensive (€800-1500+). But for a race you've trained months for, the performance and comfort benefits outweigh this risk.

**Drawback of aluminium:** Some aluminium frames (like Cube Attain SLX) are very comfortable for their price. The comfort gap has narrowed with better tube shaping and carbon forks. If budget is tight, good aluminium + carbon fork + big tyres is a valid compromise.

## 2. Groupset: Electronic (Di2/AXS) vs Mechanical

| Factor | Electronic (Di2/AXS) | Mechanical (cable) |
|---|---|---|
| **Shifting precision** | Perfect every time | Good, but cable stretch happens |
| **Effort to shift** | Button press — finger flick | Lever push — more effort when tired |
| **Climbing shifts** | Shifts under load better | Can skip under heavy power |
| **Maintenance** | Charge battery every 2-3 weeks | Replace cables yearly (€50) |
| **Weight** | ~200g heavier (battery + wires) | Slightly lighter |
| **Reliability** | Electronic failure is rare but happens | Cable snap is also rare |
| **Cost** | €400-800 premium over mechanical | Cheaper |
| **Crash damage** | Derailleur/hanger damage = expensive repair | Simpler, cheaper to fix |
| **Battery anxiety** | Need to remember to charge | Never think about it |

**What you'll actually feel on race day:**

- **Di2:** Fresh out of T1, your shifts are perfect. At km 150 when you're exhausted and hitting the final ramps of Alpe d'Huez, a single button tap drops you into your climbing gear. No thinking, no missed shifts.
- **Mechanical:** Also works fine. But when your hands are tired and cold on a descent, pushing a lever is more effort than a button. And if you didn't get a tune-up before the race, cable stretch can mean mis-shifts.

**Verdict:** Nice-to-have, not essential. If the budget choice is between Di2 and a better wheelset, take the wheels. If the budget choice is between Di2 and nothing, get the Di2 — it's a genuine quality-of-life improvement over a 5-hour bike leg.

![Shimano STI shifter diagram — mechanical (cable) vs electronic (button)](images/shimano-sti-diagram.svg)

**Common myth:** "Electronic shifting is unreliable and will fail mid-race." Reality: Di2 failure is extremely rare. The battery lasts 2,000-5,000 km. Most failures are caused by physical damage (crash) or running the battery dead. Charge it before the race and you'll be fine.

### What About Dura-Ace?

Dura-Ace is Shimano's top-tier groupset, sitting above Ultegra and 105 in the hierarchy:

| | 105 | Ultegra | Dura-Ace |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Price (groupset)** | ~€600 | ~€900 | ~€2,000 |
| **Weight** | ~2,800g | ~2,600g | ~2,400g |
| **Shifting quality** | Excellent | Excellent + slightly crisper | Excellent + marginally crisper |
| **Durability** | Very high | Very high | High (lighter = less robust) |

Dura-Ace saves ~200g over Ultegra and ~400g over 105 at **double to triple the cost**. On the Alpe d'Huez course, 200g saves roughly 40 seconds over 5 hours. Not worth €1,100+ unless you're racing for a podium. 105 or Ultegra is the right choice for an Ironman where comfort and reliability matter more than marginal grams.

## 3. Brakes: Disc vs Rim

![Hydraulic disc brake diagram (animated)](images/disc-brake-diagram.gif)
![Rim brake (V-brake) diagram](images/rim-brake-drawing.jpg)

| Factor | Hydraulic Disc | Rim Brake |
|---|---|---|
| **Stopping power** | Excellent — 1-finger braking | Good — 2-finger braking |
| **Wet performance** | Same as dry | 50% less power in rain |
| **Alpine descents** | Consistent, no fade | Fade possible on long descents |
| **Tyre clearance** | 30-45mm possible | 25-28mm max |
| **Wheel rub** | None (rotor based) | Can rub on carbon rims |
| **Weight** | ~300-500g heavier | Lighter |
| **Maintenance** | Bleed every 1-2 years | Replace pads (easy) |
| **Crash damage** | Rotors bend — easy fix | Rim stays true |
| **Cost** | More expensive | Cheaper |

**What this means for Alpe d'Huez:**

The course has **3 major descents**: from Col de Sarenne (13km), from Col d'Ornon (14km), and the famous Alpe d'Huez descent on the run (wait, wrong direction). Actually, you *climb* Alpe d'Huez — but you descend the other passes.

On a wet alpine descent at 70km/h, disc brakes are **significantly safer**. Rim brakes in the rain take 2x the stopping distance. If there's any chance of rain on race day, discs are non-negotiable.

**Drawback of discs:** Slightly heavier. On a 13.8km climb, 300-500g costs you about 10-15 seconds. Worth it for the safety gain on descents.

**Drawback of rim brakes:** More than a drawback — actually dangerous on wet alpine descents. If you buy a used TT bike with rim brakes, factor in extra braking distance and consider upgrading brake pads to SwissStop or Kool-Stop carbon compounds.

## 4. Tyre Clearance

| Clearance | What fits | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| **25-28mm** | Standard road tyres | Racing on smooth tarmac |
| **30-32mm** | Medium road tyres | Comfort + speed balance |
| **34-38mm** | Wide road tyres | Rough roads, extra comfort |
| **40-45mm** | All-road/gravel tyres | Mixed surfaces, max comfort |
| **Cube Attain C:62** | Max **34mm** | Good for a pure road bike |
| **Giant Defy Advanced** | Max **40mm** | Significant comfort advantage |

**What wider tyres actually do:**

On rough alpine roads, a 35mm tyre at lower pressure:
- **Vibrates 30% less** than a 28mm at the same pressure
- **Corner grip is higher** — more rubber on the road
- **Rolling resistance is the same** at the right pressure (modern 32mm is as fast as 28mm)
- **Puncture resistance improves** — less pinch flats

**Drawback of narrow clearance (Cube Attain 34mm):** You're locked into 30-32mm tyres. That's adequate but not ideal for rough roads. You can't put 35mm tyres on for extra comfort on race day.

**Drawback of wide clearance (Giant Defy 40mm):** The frame is slightly heavier and less aerodynamic with wider fork legs. On a pure fast group ride, a 28mm bike would be 5-10W faster. But at your pace and for comfort, the Defy's clearance is an advantage.

**Recommendation:** If riding comfort is your #1 priority, prioritize a bike with ≥35mm tyre clearance. The Giant Defy (40mm) is the standout here.

![Bicycle wheel cross-section — clincher (left) and tubular (right)](images/wheel-cross-sections.svg)

### Tubular vs Clincher vs Tubeless

Three tyre types you'll encounter:

| | Clincher | Tubeless | Tubular |
|---|---|---|---|
| **How it works** | Inner tube inside tyre | Tyre seals directly to rim (no tube) | Tyre glued to rim (no tube, no bead) |
| **Roadside repair** | Swap tube (5 min) | Plug hole or re-seal (10-15 min) | **Impossible** — must replace entire tyre |
| **Puncture resistance** | Moderate | Best — sealant seals small holes | Moderate |
| **Rolling resistance** | Good | Best — no tube friction | Excellent |
| **Weight** | Heavy (tube + tyre) | Light (no tube) | **Lightest** |
| **Rim compatibility** | Standard hook bead | Hookless or hooked (must match) | **Special tubular rim only** |
| **Common where** | Everywhere | Increasingly standard on new bikes | Pro racing, old-school TT bikes |
| **Cost** | Cheapest | Mid | **Most expensive** |

**Tubulars** (also called "tubs" or "sew-ups") are the traditional pro racing tyre — the tyre is stitched around the tube and glued onto a special rim. They're light and you can ride them flat to a wheel pit, but **you cannot fix a puncture roadside**. You carry a spare tubular (folded) and replace the whole tyre.

**For Alpe d'Huez:** Tubeless or clincher only. If you buy a used bike with tubular wheels, budget €300-600 to swap the wheelset. Riding a tubular race is risky — one puncture on a descent and you're walking down the mountain.

## 5. Gearing — What You Need to Climb Alpe d'Huez

Alpe d'Huez has sections at **11-13% gradient**. Here's what different gears feel like:

| Low Gear | Rider speed at 80rpm on 13% | Feeling |
|---|---|---|
| **53/39 + 11-25** (old race bike) | 6.5 km/h | Brutal — will grind your legs |
| **52/36 + 11-28** (sportive) | 7.5 km/h | Hard — you'll be standing a lot |
| **50/34 + 11-28** (compact) | 8.5 km/h | Manageable — some standing |
| **50/34 + 11-34** (Cube Attain) | **9.5 km/h** | **Comfortable — seated climbing** |
| **48/31 + 11-40** (gravel) | 11 km/h | Very easy — spinning |

**The Cube Attain's 50/34 + 11-34 gives a 1:1 ratio.** This means you can sit in the saddle and spin up the steepest sections. Your legs stay fresher for the run.

**Drawback of wide-range cassettes:** The jumps between gears are bigger. You might find yourself wishing for a gear between two sprockets at certain gradients. With 11-34, the jumps are: 11-12-13-14-16-18-21-24-28-32-34 (Shimano 105/Ultegra 12-speed). The gaps are bigger in the mid-range for hilly riding.

**If you arrive and find it's not low enough:** Swap the cassette to 11-36 (Ultegra/105 GS derailleur supports up to 36T). Cost: ~€60-100. Or swap to a 46/33 sub-compact crankset (~€300). Many Alpe d'Huez participants run 34/36 or even 30/40 setups.

**Key insight for you:** The Cube Attain and Canyon Endurace at 50/34 + 11-34 are already well-geared for Alpe d'Huez. Most used TT bikes (Speedmax, Aerium, etc.) sold used come with 52/36 + 11-28 — that is NOT enough for an Ironman pace up this climb.

## 6. Geometry — Endurance vs Race vs Tri

| Geometry | Stack/Reach ratio | Position | Best for |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Race (Cube Agree, Canyon Aeroad)** | ~1.35-1.40 | Low, stretched, aggressive | Fast group rides, crit racing |
| **Endurance (Cube Attain, Giant Defy, Canyon Endurace)** | ~1.45-1.55 | Higher, more upright | Long rides, comfort, all-day |
| **Tri/TT (Canyon Speedmax, Cube Aerium)** | N/A (steep seat tube) | Aero, forward rotation | Flat/rolling time trials |

**What endurance geometry actually does for you:**

| Measure | Race bike | Endurance bike | Difference you feel |
|---|---|---|---|
| Head tube height | 160mm (56cm) | 180-200mm (56cm) | +2-4cm higher bars |
| Reach | 395mm | 375-385mm | 1-2cm shorter |
| Seat tube angle | 73.5° | 73° | Slightly more upright |
| Wheelbase | 985mm | 1000-1015mm | More stable at speed |

**The result:** On an endurance bike after 180km, your back, neck, and shoulders will hurt less. You'll be less stretched out, more upright. For an Ironman where you still need to run a marathon after, this matters enormously.

**Drawback of endurance geometry:**
- 5-10W less aerodynamic at 35km/h (matters less on a mountainous course)
- Slightly less responsive handling (matters less at Ironman pace)
- Less "exciting" feel (but your goal is comfort, not excitement)

**Drawback of tri/TT geometry:**
- Steep seat tube (78-80°) puts your hip angle closed — harder to produce power on climbs
- Extremely aggressive aero position wrecks your lower back over 5 hours
- Poor handling on descents — the Alpe d'Huez route has technical descending
- **The Alpe d'Huez race organisers explicitly recommend against TT bikes for this course**

**Verdict:** You're making the right choice with endurance geometry. Don't be tempted by a used TT bike — it will be slower overall on this course because you lose more time on the climbs than you gain on the flats.

## 7. Wheels — Weight vs Aero

| Wheelset | Weight | Aero benefit | Best for |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Shallow alloy (25-35mm)** | ~1,800g | Minimal | Climbing, budget |
| **Mid-depth carbon (40-50mm)** | ~1,500-1,600g | Good | All-round, best value |
| **Deep carbon (60-80mm)** | ~1,500-1,700g | Excellent | Flat/rolling, windy |
| **Disc wheel (rear)** | ~1,200g | Maximum | TT/tri only |

**What matters for Alpe d'Huez:**

| Segment | Duration | Best wheel type |
|---|---|---|
| Flat/rolling (50% of course) | ~2 hours | 40-50mm carbon — aero benefit |
| Climbing (30% of course) | ~1.5 hours | Light wheels — less rotating mass |
| Descending (20% of course) | ~45 min | Stable wheels — crosswind handling |

**Best compromise:** 40-50mm carbon wheels. They're light enough to climb, aero enough for the flats, and stable in crosswinds. The Cube Attain C:62 SLX comes with Newmen Evolution SL R.35 (35mm) which are decent.

**Drawback of the stock wheels (both Cube and Giant):** They're alloy and relatively heavy (~1,800-1,900g). If you have budget leftover, upgrading to a set of mid-depth carbon wheels (e.g., used 50mm Chinese carbon or entry-level Hunt/Zerobreeze) is the single best upgrade for speed and comfort.

**What to budget:** €400-800 for used carbon wheels, or €600-1,200 for new entry-level.

### Deep Section Wheels (>60mm) — Why Skip for This Course

Deep section wheels have tall rims (60-80mm+) designed for aerodynamics. The taller the rim, the more it slices through the air at high speed — but there are trade-offs:

| Depth | Aero gain | Weight | Crosswind handling | Best use |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **25-35mm** (shallow) | Minimal | Lightest | Stable | Climbing |
| **40-55mm** (mid) | Good | Moderate | Manageable | All-round |
| **60-80mm** (deep) | Excellent | Heavy | Twitchy | Flat TT/tri |
| **Disc wheel** | Maximum | Heaviest | Very twitchy | Indoor/pursuit |

On Alpe d'Huez, **deep wheels hurt more than they help**:

- **Crosswinds on descents:** The course has exposed alpine descents where crosswinds catch deep rims like a sail. At 70km/h, a gust can push you into the next lane — dangerous on narrow mountain roads with drop-offs.
- **Weight on climbs:** Deep rims weigh 200-400g more than shallow. On 3,200m of climbing, that costs ~1-2 minutes.
- **Low-speed aero penalty:** Below 30km/h (which is most of the climbing), deep rims provide negligible aero benefit. On Alpe d'Huez, your average climbing speed will be 10-15km/h.
- **Stock Cube/Defy wheels are 35mm:** That's already adequate. Upgrade to 45-50mm if you want, but don't go deeper than 55mm.

**If you already own deep section wheels:** Swap the front to a shallower rim (40-50mm) for stability, keep a deeper rear if you want. Many riders run a 50mm front + 60mm rear combo as a compromise.

## 8. Transport & Travel — Packing for Alpe d'Huez

You're riding from the Netherlands to the French Alps. That means car, train, or plane. How easy a bike packs affects your pre-race stress.

### Wheel Removal (All Disc Bikes)

All bikes here use **thru-axles** — slide out, wheel comes off. Universal:

| Step | Time | Tool needed |
|---|---|---|
| Remove front wheel | 10 sec | 5mm/6mm hex (or lever on some) |
| Remove rear wheel | 15 sec | Same hex key |
| Reinstall | 20 sec each | None (finger-tight + ¼ turn) |

**⚠️ Critical for disc brakes:** When the wheel is off, **do not squeeze the brake lever** — the caliper pistons will push out and may need resetting. Use the plastic **pad spacer** that came with the bike. If lost, fold a thick business card between the pads.

### Cockpit: Integrated vs Standard

| Type | Example bikes | Packing ease |
|---|---|---|
| **Standard stem + round bars** | Cube Attain, Giant Defy, Merida Scultura, Koga Roqa | ★★★★★ — remove 4 bolts, bars come off. 5 min |
| **Semi-integrated (cables hidden)** | Cannondale Synapse, Canyon Endurace | ★★★★☆ — cables run into stem, need to disconnect. 10 min |
| **Fully integrated (Canyon one-piece)** | Canyon Endurace CF SLX (some models) | ★★★☆☆ — proprietary, need to mark position, harder to adjust |

**Bike case tip:** Standard round bars can be turned sideways in the case. Wide flare gravel bars (Koga 45mm) need a wider case.

### Seatpost: Round vs Aero

| Shape | Example | How to pack |
|---|---|---|
| **Round 27.2mm** | Cube Attain C:62 SLX/Race, Merida Scultura | ★★★★★ — loosen clamp, slide down. Mark height with tape. |
| **Round 27.2mm (carbon)** | Cube, Cannondale Synapse | ★★★★☆ — same but use torque wrench on reassembly |
| **Round 31.8mm** | Koga Roqa | ★★★★★ — same as 27.2mm |
| **D-shaped** | Giant Defy (D-Fuse) | ★★★☆☆ — must mark exact rotation, clamp is specific |
| **Special (VCLS 2-piece)** | Canyon Endurace | ★★★★☆ — flip-head mechanism, note the position |

### Frame Material & Travel

| Material | Airline risk | Car/train | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Carbon** | ✅ OK in hard case; fragile in soft bag | ✅ Fine | Inspect after every flight. Hard case recommended (€200-500) |
| **Aluminium** | ✅ Tougher, dents rather than cracks | ✅ Fine | More forgiving if the case takes a hit |

### Easy Workarounds for the "Harder" Bikes

In practice, **none of these bikes are actually hard to transport** — the differences are small. Here are the simple fixes for each quirk:

| "Problem" | Workaround | Time | Cost |
|---|---|---|---|
| **D-Fuse seatpost (Giant Defy)** | Put a strip of electrical tape at the exposed height mark. 2 seconds. On reassembly, line up the tape. | 2 sec | €0 |
| **IsoSpeed (Trek Domane)** | You don't need to touch it. Just remove the seatpost normally. IsoSpeed sits above the clamp. | 0 sec | €0 |
| **VCLS flip-head (Canyon Endurace)** | Draw a line across the joint with a Sharpie before loosening. Re-align on assembly. | 5 sec | €0 |
| **SmartSense lights (Cannondale)** | Leave them in the frame. The lights are embedded — no need to disconnect for wheel removal. | 0 sec | €0 |
| **Carbon frame (all carbon bikes)** | Buy a hard-shell travel case (€150-300 used on Marktplaats). Soft bags are risky for carbon. | — | €150-300 |
| **Heavy bike (9.3 kg Merida)** | A 0.9 kg difference means nothing when you're carrying a bike case. Lift with your legs. | — | €0 |
| **Lost disc pad spacer** | Fold a thick business card or piece of corrugated cardboard to 3mm thickness. Wedge between pads. | 30 sec | €0 |
| **Integrated cockpit (Canyon)** | Most bike travel cases (e.g., B&W, Scicon, Thule) allow you to leave the bars on — just turn them sideways and pad. | — | €0 |
| **No torque wrench at destination** | Tighten to "firm but not cranking" feel, then visit any local bike shop for a proper check (€10-15). | — | €10-15 |

**Bottom line:** Don't let "transport difficulty" drive your bike choice. All of these bikes pack in 15-20 minutes once you've done it once. The differences are minor quirks with trivial workarounds.

## 9. Weight — Obsessing Over Grams

| Bike weight | Time penalty on Alpe d'Huez (vs 7.5kg) |
|---|---|
| **7.5 kg** (pro bike) | Baseline |
| **8.0 kg** (Canyon Endurace CF SL Di2) | +45 seconds |
| **8.4 kg** (Cube Attain C:62 SLX) | +1 minute 30 seconds |
| **8.7 kg** (Giant Defy Advanced 1) | +2 minutes |
| **9.2 kg** (Cube Attain C:62 Race) | +3 minutes |
| **9.6 kg** (Cube Attain SLX aluminium) | +4 minutes |

On the full 118km course, weight matters about **2 seconds per kg per km of climbing**. Over 3,200m of elevation gain, each kg costs roughly 4-5 minutes total.

**But here's the reality:** The difference between 8.4 kg and 9.2 kg is ~3 minutes over 5 hours of riding. Meanwhile, a 10W aero improvement at 35km/h saves 4-5 minutes. A proper bike fit saves time AND feels better.

**Don't over-obsess about weight.** Comfort, fit, and gearing matter more for your Ironman than shaving 500g off the bike weight. The heaviest bike in this comparison (9.6 kg aluminium) would cost you maybe 5 minutes over the lightest (8.0 kg carbon). That's a drop in the bucket on a 12-14 hour race day.

## 10. Summary — What to Prioritize for YOUR Race

### Must-Have (non-negotiable)

| Priority | Why |
|---|---|
| **Hydraulic disc brakes** | Alpine descents, possible rain, safety at 70km/h |
| **Endurance geometry** | Back/neck comfort over 5h, then 42km run |
| **50/34 or lower gearing** | Need ≤1:1 ratio for 13% gradients on Alpe d'Huez |
| **30mm+ tyre clearance** | Comfort on rough alpine roads |

### Nice-to-Have (worth the money)

| Priority | Why |
|---|---|
| **Carbon frame** | Vibration damping, lighter, less fatigue |
| **Electronic shifting** | Perfect shifts when exhausted, no misfires |
| **≥35mm tyre clearance** | Can run 32-35mm tyres for max comfort |
| **Good wheels** | 40-50mm depth, ~1,600g or less |

### Nice-to-Have (only if budget allows)

| Item | Cost | Benefit |
|---|---|---|
| **Power meter** | €250-600 | Pacing on climbs — prevents blowing up |
| **Carbon wheels** | €500-1,200 | Aero + weight savings |
| **Clip-on aero bars** | €50-200 | ~10-15 minutes saved over 180km |
| **Professional bike fit** | €150-250 | Prevents injury, improves comfort |

### Skip (not worth it for this race)

| Item | Why |
|---|---|
| **TT/Tri bike** | Heavier, worse climbing, worse descending, uncomfortable |
| **Deep section wheels (>60mm)** | See §7 — twitchy in alpine crosswinds, heavy on climbs, <30km/h aero penalty |
| **Dura-Ace over Ultegra/105** | See §2 — €1,100+ extra for ~40 seconds over 5h. Not worth it |
| **Tubular tyres** | See §4 — glued to rim, no roadside repair. Budget €300-600 to swap wheels |
| **The lightest possible build** | Shaving 500g costs €1000+ for 2 minutes gain |
